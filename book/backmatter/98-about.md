# About this book

## The sources

This is a book of commentary. It exists to send you to the originals, not to replace them. Each seminar closes with a reading list; the first item is always the primary source, and it is the one to read. The papers and theses discussed here are, with few exceptions, freely available online, and they are more approachable than their reputations suggest once you know the problem they were solving. That is what these seminars are for.

## The method

Every chapter was written from the primary source, with the key definitions, enumerations, and figures quoted verbatim rather than recalled. Then every chapter was put through an adversarial review panel: reviewers taking the roles of a distributed-systems engineer, a programming-languages researcher, a reliability engineer, a systems historian, and others, whose job was to attack the claims, catch folklore, reject shallow analogies, and refuse any modern idea wrongly attributed to a historical author. The test each chapter had to pass was simple: if the original author read it, would they recognize their own idea and also learn something from the modern reading? Chapters that could not pass were rewritten.

The reviewers disagreed with each other often. That was the point. The goal was never consensus, only honesty about what these works do and do not say.
